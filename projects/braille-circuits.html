TEKNOFEST Accessibility Education

Braille Circuits

An accessibility-focused educational project that uses tactile Braille-based representations to help visually impaired individuals understand electrical circuits and meaningfully engage with STEM education.

●  Educational Project · TEKNOFEST

Overview

Braille Circuits addresses a persistent gap in STEM education: the near-total exclusion of visually impaired students from hands-on electrical circuit learning. Standard circuit diagrams, breadboards, and laboratory equipment are designed for sighted users, leaving students who are blind or have low vision without access to fundamental engineering concepts that are core to modern technology.

The project creates a tactile learning system that encodes circuit components, connections, and diagrams in Braille-compatible formats, allowing visually impaired students to explore, understand, and work with electrical circuits through touch rather than sight.

Key Aspects

  • Tactile representations of resistors, capacitors, LEDs, switches, and other standard components
  • Braille-annotated circuit diagrams for common circuit configurations
  • Educational kit design structured around progressive learning objectives
  • Covers series circuits, parallel circuits, Ohm's law, and basic electronics
  • Designed to integrate with existing STEM curricula
  • Inclusive design methodology — developed with accessibility as a first principle

Why It Matters

Electrical circuits are foundational to physics, electronics, and engineering education. Students who cannot access this content are effectively barred from entire pathways in science and technology. Braille Circuits is not an accommodation or a workaround — it is a purpose-built educational tool that treats accessibility as the design constraint, not an afterthought.

The project reflects Terra Reform's broader commitment to ensuring that innovation and STEM education serve all students, regardless of ability.

Program Context

Developed for TEKNOFEST, the project competed in tracks focused on educational technology and social innovation, where accessibility and inclusive design were central evaluation criteria.
